Penalty Kick and Out of Bounds

(Throw-in, Goal Kick, Corner Kick)

Penalty Kick:

  • A penalty kick is awarded to the attacking team if a defending player commits a foul inside their own penalty area.

  • The penalty kick is taken from the penalty spot, which is located 11 meters (12 yards) from the goal line.

  • Only the goalkeeper is allowed to defend the goal during a penalty kick, and all other players must be outside the penalty area and penalty arc until the ball is kicked.

    • Out of Bounds (Throw-in, Goal Kick, Corner Kick):

  • When the ball crosses the side touchlines, a throw-in is awarded to the opposing team. A player from the team that did not touch the ball last throws it back into play.

  • If the attacking team plays the ball out over the defending team's goal line, a goal kick is awarded to the defending team. The goal kick is taken from anywhere within the goal area.

  • If the defending team plays the ball out over its own goal line, a corner kick is awarded to the attacking team. The corner kick is taken from the corner arc closest to where the ball went out.

    • These rules help ensure fair play and provide a method for restarting the game in different situations. Throw-ins, goal kicks, and corner kicks are ways to resume play after the ball goes out of bounds, while penalty kicks are a direct opportunity for a team to score after a serious foul occurs inside the penalty area.

    Referee

    Cards

    Red and Yellow card
  • Yellow Card (Caution):

    • A yellow card is shown by the referee to caution a player for various offenses, such as reckless tackles, dissent, or persistent fouling. When a player receives two yellow cards in the same match, it results in an automatic red card, leading to their expulsion from the game.

  • Red Card (Sending Off):

    • A red card is a more severe penalty, leading to a player's immediate expulsion from the game. It can be shown for serious offenses like violent conduct, serious foul play, or denying an obvious goal-scoring opportunity through a foul. A team playing with fewer than 11 players due to a red card may face a significant disadvantage.

  • Direct and Indirect Free Kicks:

    • A direct free kick is awarded to the opposing team when a player receives a yellow or red card. If the offense is deemed more severe (resulting in a red card), the opposing team may be awarded an indirect free kick from the spot of the offense.

  • Disciplinary Consequences:

    • Accumulating yellow cards over multiple matches can lead to suspension for subsequent games. Red cards often result in more extended suspensions, depending on the severity of the offense.

    The card system is an essential part of the game, helping referees maintain order on the field and ensuring fair play. It acts as a deterrent against reckless or dangerous behavior, promoting sportsmanship and the safety of players. The severity of the penalty (yellow or red card) is determined by the nature and impact of the player's actions on the game.

    Offside

    Offside rules
  • Offside Position:

    • A player is in an offside position if they are nearer to the opponent's goal line than both the ball and the second-to-last defender (usually the last outfield player) when the ball is played to them.

  • Active Involvement:

    • Simply being in an offside position is not an offense. The player must also be actively involved in the play. This includes receiving the ball, interfering with an opponent, or gaining an advantage from being in that position.

  • Exceptions:

    • Players are not in an offside position if they are in their own half of the field of play or level with the second-to-last opponent or level with the last two opponents. Additionally, players cannot be offside when receiving the ball directly from a goal kick, corner kick, or throw-in.

  • Offside Offense:

    • A player is penalized for being offside only when actively participating in the play or gaining an advantage from their position. When an offside offense occurs, the opposing team is awarded a free-kick from the spot where the offside player was at the time the ball was played to them.

    The offside rule aims to encourage fair and competitive play by ensuring that attacking players cannot exploit their position to gain an unfair advantage over the defending team. It adds an element of strategy and timing to offensive plays, requiring players to carefully judge their positions relative to the ball and the opposing defenders.
